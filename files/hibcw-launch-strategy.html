The Foundation of Every Message

Our Story

Before any post goes out, every member of the team needs to know this history — because it is the most compelling thing about HIBCW. This is not backstory. This is the headline.

The through-line: KIAA was born from a wave, built on resilience, and has never stopped fighting for Hawaii Island businesses. HIBCW is not a new idea — it is the same idea with 21st-century tools.
May 23, 1960
The Tsunami That Changed Hilo
A magnitude 9.5 earthquake off the coast of Chile triggered a tsunami that struck Hilo with devastating force. The Hilo waterfront — home to many of the city's businesses — was destroyed. Dozens of businesses lost everything overnight. The community was left to rebuild from the ground up.
Hilo Waterfront Community Displaced
1960s
Businesses Rebuild — And Band Together
Displaced businesses relocated to what is now the Kanoelehua Industrial Area of Hilo. Once there, they faced a new challenge: crime in their new location. Rather than wait for someone else to solve it, these businesses stood together. KIAA was founded to curtail the crime plaguing their rebuilt community, built on the principle that collective action is the most powerful safety tool there is.
KIAA Founded Kanoelehua Industrial Area
1990s
KIAA + KTA: A Police Sub Station
Crime in the industrial area persisted. KIAA and KTA Super Stores co-sponsored a Police Sub Station right in the Kanoelehua Industrial Area — a direct, community-funded response. This was not a government initiative handed down from above. It was neighbors and businesses pooling resources to protect their own. The same spirit that founded KIAA in the 1960s was alive three decades later.
KIAA + KTA Partnership Police Sub Station · Kanoelehua
Today
HIBCW — The Mission Goes Island-Wide
HIBCW is the latest chapter in a story that started on the Hilo waterfront in 1960. What began as a handful of displaced businesses protecting one industrial area now covers all 9 districts of Hawaii Island — Hamakua to North Kohala, Hilo to South Kona. The tools have changed. The commitment has not.

Approved Brand Lines

Use These Across All Channels

Primary — Formal Use

"Born from a wave. Built to last."

Lead line for all formal materials: press releases, signage, website about page, printed flyers, and the YouTube video opening. Short, earned, and impossible to argue with.

Secondary — Social & Email

"Same community. Same mission. New tools."

Use in email subject lines, Instagram captions, and LinkedIn posts. Works as a standalone closing line at the end of any post to tie it back to the origin story without re-telling it every time.

Community Voice — Nextdoor & Member Email

"We've seen what happens when businesses stand alone."

Use where a personal, direct tone fits. Speaks to the original motivation behind KIAA and makes the case for joining HIBCW without ever having to say "join HIBCW."

Full Credential — New Audiences

"KIAA has been protecting Hawaii Island businesses since 1960. HIBCW is how we do it today."

Use when introducing HIBCW to audiences unfamiliar with KIAA — chamber events, press releases, new partner outreach. The sentence earns the credibility before making the ask.

Why History Wins

What This Story Does for the Campaign

Instant Credibility

No Track Record Needed

HIBCW is a new platform, but KIAA is not. A new app asking for trust has to earn it over time. KIAA asking for trust already has 60+ years of it. Lead with the organization, not the technology.

Emotional Resonance

A Story People Remember

The 1960 tsunami is known to every long-time Hawaii Island resident. Connecting HIBCW to that origin story makes it memorable in a way a feature list never could. People share stories. They don't share feature lists.

True Differentiation

No App Can Copy This

Any tech company can build a crime reporting app. No tech company can claim 60 years of community service on Hawaii Island. This history is HIBCW's only truly inimitable asset — use it in every message.

Phase 1 · Weeks 1–4

KIAA Soft Launch

Before going public, bring your existing KIAA member network onboard. They are your founding members, social proof, and word-of-mouth engine — and they already know and trust the KIAA name. Lead every touchpoint with the history.

Why soft launch first? HIBCW's value compounds with network size. If the public visits the map on Day 1 and sees zero incidents, they will leave. By seeding KIAA members first, you build an active data layer before the public launch — so when island-wide promotion begins, the map is alive and credible. Your members also become ambassadors who can say "I've been using it since day one."

Send the history-anchored email (see Post Templates tab) to your full KIAA member list. Subject line: "60 years ago, KIAA was born from a crisis. We're still at it." Tracking Setup

What to Measure & How

Website Traffic

Google Analytics (Free)

Add Google Analytics 4 to hibcw.kiaahilo.org to track page views, session source (email vs Instagram vs Nextdoor vs direct), and which pages visitors land on. This tells you which channels are driving actual traffic vs. just impressions.

Link Tracking

UTM Parameters

Add ?utm_source=instagram, ?utm_source=nextdoor, etc. to every URL posted on social. Analytics then shows you exactly which post drove which visits. Free. Takes 30 seconds per link to set up.

Email

Open Rate + Click Rate

Track via Mailchimp, Constant Contact, or whatever platform KIAA uses. Goal: 35%+ open rate (very achievable with a warm member list). Click rate goal: 10%+ on the launch email. The history-anchored subject line should outperform any generic alternative.

Social

Reach & Saves (not Likes)

On Instagram, track Reach and Saves — not Likes. Saves signal the content was useful enough to return to. On LinkedIn, track impressions and profile visits. On Nextdoor, track replies and direct messages from business owners asking how to join.

Monthly Reporting Cadence: At the end of each month, compile a one-page report: total registered businesses, new registrations this month, top-performing post, website sessions by source, and one qualitative win (member story, press mention, captain note). Share with KIAA leadership to maintain organizational buy-in and keep all 9 district captains engaged in the program.
